# Drone Regulations Guide for Chinese Tourist (DJI Consumer Drone)
# 无人机携带与飞行法规指南 — 法德瑞意四国

**Applicable dates:** 2026.3.31 - 4.13
**Drone type assumed:** DJI consumer drone (e.g., DJI Mini 4 Pro <249g, or DJI Air series ~595-720g)
**Operator nationality:** Chinese citizen (non-EU)

> **IMPORTANT NOTE ON DRONE WEIGHT:** If you bring a sub-249g drone (DJI Mini series), some registration and insurance requirements are lighter. If your drone is 250g+, nearly all requirements apply in full. The DJI Mini 4 Pro (249g) sits right on the boundary — some countries treat it as 250g class. Check your specific model's weight carefully.

---

## Overall EU Framework (Applies to France, Germany, Italy)

The EU uses a harmonized drone regulation system under EASA (European Union Aviation Safety Agency):

- **Open Category** is what recreational/tourist flights fall under
  - **A1 subcategory:** Fly over people (drones <250g only)
  - **A2 subcategory:** Fly close to people (requires additional certificate)
  - **A3 subcategory:** Fly far from people (drones <25kg, must stay 150m from residential/commercial/industrial areas)
- **Maximum altitude:** 120 meters (400 feet) above ground level
- **Visual Line of Sight (VLOS)** required at all times
- **Non-EU tourists** must register as a UAS operator in whichever EU country they first fly in
- **Each country adds its own national rules** on top of the EU framework
- **Switzerland** is NOT in the EU but follows EASA rules through bilateral agreements

### Detailed: LF-P 23 Zone and Flying Near Paris (Updated March 2026)

**Q: Is there ANY way to fly a sub-249g drone near Paris?**

**No, not within the LF-P 23 zone.** The sub-249g weight exemption does NOT override local prohibited zones. Even a DJI Mini is explicitly banned within Paris. The LF-P 23 zone covers the entire area within the boulevards des marechaux / peripherique ring road, from ground level to 6,500 feet. The zone was renewed by an order dated April 2, 2025 (published in the Official Journal April 5, 2025), confirming its permanent status.

**Key 2025 change:** The advance application period for professional authorization was doubled from 5 to 10 working days, making even permitted professional flights harder to arrange.

**Parks and outskirts:** Bois de Boulogne, Jardin du Luxembourg, and all other green spaces within Paris are prohibited. There are no "hidden" flyable parks within the city.

**Just outside the LF-P 23 zone:** The immediate suburbs (petite couronne) have their own layered restrictions from Charles de Gaulle, Orly, and Le Bourget airports, plus military zones. You would need to travel well outside the Ile-de-France region to find reliably open airspace. Model aircraft clubs in the Ile-de-France region offer controlled areas for members, but these are not practical for tourists.

**Q: What about the countryside between Paris and Belgium/Germany (Eurostar/TGV route)?**

The northern countryside through Picardy and Champagne is generally MORE permissive than Paris. In rural agricultural areas away from towns, airports, and protected zones, flying a sub-249g drone is legally possible provided you:
- Are registered on AlphaTango (required because the Mini 4 Pro has a camera)
- Have insurance
- Check the Geoportail map for the specific location
- Stay below 120m, maintain VLOS, avoid built-up areas

However, for your itinerary this is impractical — you are traveling by train (not driving) through these areas, and there are no planned stops in the rural countryside.

**Potentially flyable areas near your route (if you had time):**

- Rural Picardy fields
- Countryside in Normandy, Loire Valley, or Burgundy (none on your route)

## 2. GERMANY (Detailed Research — March 2026)

### Can a Chinese tourist legally fly a sub-249g DJI Mini 4 Pro?

**Yes, but registration is still required because the DJI Mini 4 Pro has a camera.**

Under EU/EASA rules (which Germany follows), the sub-250g weight exemption from registration ONLY applies if the drone has **no camera or sensor capable of capturing personal data**. Since the DJI Mini 4 Pro has a camera, the operator **must register** regardless of the drone being under 250g. This is explicitly stated in EU Implementing Regulation 2019/947 Article 14 and confirmed on the German DIPUL portal.

**What you DO get from being sub-249g:**
- Flies in **A1 Open Category** — allowed to fly near (and even over) uninvolved persons (but never over crowds/assemblies)
- **No pilot competency certificate (A1/A3) required** for sub-250g drones used privately (non-commercial)
- **No Remote ID broadcast required** under current rules (though this may change — see regulation changes below)
- Fewer operational restrictions than heavier drones

### Registration Process on DIPUL for Non-EU Citizens

Per EASA rules (confirmed on the [EASA travelling with drones page](https://www.easa.europa.eu/en/light/topics/travelling-drones)):

> "Non-EASA residents must register as a drone operator with the National Aviation Authority of the first EASA state you intend to operate from."

**For Germany, the process is:**

1. **Go to:** https://uas-registration.lba-openuav.de/ (the LBA's online registration portal, accessed via DIPUL)
2. **Fee:** EUR 20 for natural persons (one-time)
3. **Required information:** Personal details (name, address, contact info) + proof of valid drone liability insurance
4. **Language:** The registration form is available in **German only** — use browser translation
5. **Result:** You receive an electronic identification number (e-ID) that must be physically displayed on your drone (a label/sticker)
6. **Validity:** One e-ID covers all your drones; registration valid across all EASA member states (so it also covers France, Italy, Switzerland)

**Important practical notes:**
- Non-EU citizens CAN register — EASA explicitly provides for this
- You need a valid address (your hotel address in Germany should work)
- You must have insurance BEFORE registering (insurance proof is a prerequisite)
- Training/qualifications obtained outside the EU are NOT recognized — but for sub-250g private use, no qualification is needed anyway
- **Register BEFORE your trip if possible** — the online form can be completed remotely

### Insurance Requirements for Sub-249g Drones

**Mandatory for ALL drones in Germany — including sub-249g models. No exceptions.**

This is established by German Aviation Act (LuftVG) §37 and §43:

- **Minimum coverage:** 750,000 Special Drawing Rights (~EUR 900,000-940,000). Recommended: EUR 1,000,000+
- **Proof required:** You must carry your insurance certificate while flying. Authorities can ask to see it at any time.
- **Penalty for no insurance:** Administrative offense with fines
- **This applies to all operators** — residents, visitors, tourists, commercial, recreational, any drone weight

**How to get insurance as a Chinese tourist:**
- **Option 1 (recommended):** Purchase a short-term EU drone liability policy from a German/EU provider before departure. Providers like DMO (Deutsche Modellsport Organisation), Getsafe, or piecarte.com offer policies starting from ~EUR 3.77/month
- **Option 2:** Check if your existing Chinese drone insurance covers EU operations (unlikely but worth checking)
- **Option 3:** Some travel insurance add-ons cover drone liability — verify the coverage amount meets the EUR 900K minimum
- Budget: approximately **EUR 30-100 for an annual hobby policy**, or less for short-term

### Detailed Location Analysis

#### Neuschwanstein Castle Area — COMPLETELY BANNED

The **Bavarian Palace Administration (Bayerische Schlösserverwaltung)** exercises a blanket drone ban across ALL its properties, including Neuschwanstein. This is a triple-layered prohibition:

1. **Property owner's rights (§903 BGB):** The Palace Administration prohibits flying drones over or launching from its land. This covers the castle grounds and surrounding administered areas.
2. **Nature reserve:** Neuschwanstein sits adjacent to/within a protected nature reserve (Naturschutzgebiet). Drone flights are banned over nature reserves for sport or recreation.
3. **Cultural heritage protection:** The castle is a major heritage monument.

**Penalties:** Up to EUR 50,000 fine for aviation violations in protected zones. Additionally, strict liability for any damage — even unintentional.

**Practical note:** There are NO legal launch points near the castle. The surrounding area is a mix of Palace Administration land, nature reserve, and the Marienbrücke (bridge) area which is always crowded. Even the famous viewpoints overlook restricted airspace. **Do not attempt to fly here.**

#### Rural Bavaria (Farmland between Munich and Neuschwanstein) — BEST OPPORTUNITY

This is your most realistic flying location in Germany:

- The B17/A7 route from Munich to Füssen passes through open Bavarian farmland
- Much of this is **not** in a nature reserve or restricted zone
- **You MUST check the DFS DrohnenApp or DIPUL map** before every flight — scattered nature reserves, bird sanctuaries, and Natura 2000 areas exist along the route
- **Rules for rural flying:** Stay below 120m, maintain visual line of sight, do not fly over people or buildings without consent, keep 1.5km from airports/airfields
- **Tip:** Look for open agricultural fields well away from villages. The Pfaffenwinkel (area south of Munich, north of Füssen) has beautiful rolling farmland with Alpine views that could make for excellent footage

#### Ramsau bei Berchtesgaden — St. Sebastian Church — TECHNICALLY POSSIBLE BUT RISKY

**Key finding: The village of Ramsau is OUTSIDE the Berchtesgaden National Park boundary.**

The national park boundary runs south of the village. The famous Pfarrkirche St. Sebastian (the iconic church with the mountain backdrop) is in the village proper, outside the park.

**However, multiple restrictions still apply:**
- The village is within the **municipality** that hosts part of the national park — local authorities are extremely sensitive to drone activity
- You would be flying over/near a **church** (cultural/religious building) and near **residential properties** — German privacy law prohibits flying camera drones over residential areas without consent
- The Ramsauer Ache (stream) area around the church is a popular tourist spot — flying over uninvolved persons in crowds is prohibited even with sub-250g drones
- There may be local **Natura 2000 / FFH designations** even outside the national park — check the DIPUL map carefully
- **Enforcement reality:** Local authorities in the Berchtesgaden area actively enforce drone bans given the proximity to the national park. Police may not distinguish between "technically outside the park" and "near the park."

**Verdict:** Legally possible in theory if you find a launch point away from buildings, people, and protected areas. In practice, the area around the famous church viewpoint is too crowded and too close to residential buildings. An early morning flight (before tourists arrive) from an open field NEAR the church — not directly over it — might work, but carries real enforcement risk.

#### Königssee — MOSTLY BANNED, with a small exception

**The majority of Königssee is inside Berchtesgaden National Park = drone ban.**

However, based on the park boundary research:

- The **northern end of Königssee** (near Schönau am Königssee, where the boat dock/parking area is) is **outside** the national park boundary
- The park boundary runs from south of Hintersee eastward along the northern slopes of Hochkalter and Watzmann to Jenner, **excluding the northern tip of the lake**
- **Schönau am Königssee** (the town at the north end) is partially outside the park

**But practically:**
- The northern shore area is the **busiest tourist area** (boat dock, restaurants, parking) — flying over crowds is banned
- The town has residential buildings — privacy restrictions apply
- Even over the "outside the park" portion of the lake, you are likely within the **visual range of the park** and may attract enforcement attention
- Some sources note that "theoretically, flying drones over a part of the lake would be allowed" but recommend checking with the local tourist office

**Verdict:** There is a narrow legal gray zone at the very northern tip of the lake, but it is not practically useful for good footage (the dramatic scenery is deeper in the park). **Not recommended.**

### Night Flying
Permitted with a green flashing anti-collision light (more lenient than France).

### Penalties
- Administrative fines: EUR 500 to EUR 50,000
- Endangering air traffic: up to 10 years imprisonment
- Nature reserve violations: up to EUR 50,000
- Strict liability for all damage (even unintentional)

### 2025-2026 Regulation Changes

**Current rules (as of March 2026) have NOT relaxed requirements for sub-250g drones. In fact, rules may get STRICTER:**

- **Proposed 100g registration threshold:** In February 2026, the EU Commission published a Drone Security Package proposing to lower the registration threshold from 250g to **100g**. If approved (targeted Q3 2026), this would mean ALL drones over 100g need registration — the sub-250g "light touch" advantage would shrink significantly. This mirrors the UK, which already dropped its threshold to 100g in January 2026.
- **Remote ID expansion:** EASA is expanding Remote ID requirements. Currently sub-250g C0 drones are mostly exempt, but this may change.
- **U-space (digital airspace management):** Rolling out across the EU in 2025-2026. This automated system will eventually require drones to connect to airspace management services in urban/controlled areas.
- **No relaxation for sub-250g:** Despite hopes, the EU has NOT removed the registration requirement for camera-equipped sub-250g drones. The trend is toward MORE regulation, not less.

**Bottom line for your April 2026 trip:** The current rules apply. The 100g threshold change, if approved, would likely not take effect before your trip.

## 3. SWITZERLAND (Detailed Research — Updated March 2026)

### Can tourists fly drones?
Yes, with registration. Switzerland officially adopted EU drone regulations (EASA) on **January 1, 2023**, introducing the Open/Specific/Certified category system. The Alps offer stunning drone footage potential, BUT popular tourist areas in the Bernese Oberland are heavily restricted.

---

### 3.1 EASA Harmonization — What Changed

- Switzerland now follows **EU Regulation 2019/947** (same as EU member states)
- Three operational categories: **Open** (recreational), Specific, Certified
- Maximum altitude reduced from 150m to **120m above ground level**
- Swiss pilot certificates carry EU recognition across all EASA member states
- If you are registered in ANY EASA member state, your registration is valid in Switzerland (and vice versa)
- Switzerland started accepting **European Standard Scenarios (EU-STS)** for specific operations since January 2024

**Cross-registration benefit:** If you already registered in Germany (DIPUL, EUR 20), that registration IS valid in Switzerland. You do NOT need to register separately with FOCA. However, you still must follow Swiss-specific geographical restrictions and local rules.

### 3.2 Registration Requirements for Sub-249g DJI Mini 4 Pro

**Key distinction:** The DJI Mini 4 Pro weighs under 250g BUT has a camera. Under EASA rules, **any drone with a camera (capable of collecting personal data) requires operator registration**, regardless of weight.

- **Registration platform:** FOCA dLIS (Digital License Application System)
- **Registration fee:** CHF 10
- **Registration validity:** Unlimited period, valid in all EASA member states
- **Operator number format:** CHExxxxxxxxxxxxx (must be physically displayed on the drone — plaque, engraving, or waterproof marker in capital letters)
- **Minimum age:** 12 years (under 16 needs legal representative)

**A1/A3 online exam:** Required for drones with cameras. The exam is available on the dLIS platform. Budget approximately 4 hours to complete.

### 3.3 Can a Chinese Tourist Register on FOCA dLIS? — Actual Process

**Yes.** FOCA explicitly states: *"Persons residing outside the EASA member states who wish to operate drones in Switzerland must register in Switzerland."*

**Step-by-step process:**

1. **Create an eIAM account (AGOV/CH-Login)** at the Swiss government authentication portal
   - This is Switzerland's public digital identity system
   - Foreign nationals CAN create an account — you need a valid email address
   - **Do this well in advance of your trip** — the CH-Login setup may take time for non-residents
2. **Log into FOCA dLIS** using your eIAM/CH-Login credentials
3. **Provide required information:**
   - Surname, first name, home address, date of birth, telephone number
   - Photo or scan of passport or ID
   - For sub-250g drones: enter **"dronebelow250g"** in the insurance fields
4. **Complete A1/A3 online exam** on the dLIS platform
5. **Receive your CHE operator number** and affix it to your drone
6. **Pay CHF 10 registration fee**

**Important notes for Chinese tourists:**
- Your Chinese drone license/CAAC registration is NOT recognized in Switzerland
- The process is the same as for any non-EASA resident
- Registration is done entirely online — no need to visit a Swiss office
- **Start the process at least 2-3 weeks before travel** to allow time for account setup and exam completion
- **Alternative:** If you already registered in Germany (DIPUL), skip Swiss registration — your German e-ID is valid in Switzerland under EASA mutual recognition

### 3.4 Insurance Requirements for Sub-249g Drones

**Sub-249g drones (including DJI Mini 4 Pro) are NOT legally required to carry liability insurance in Switzerland.**

This is a significant advantage over Germany (where insurance is mandatory for ALL drones):
- FOCA **strongly recommends** coverage even for lightweight drones, noting that accident costs can rise considerably
- If you do get insurance, the recommended minimum is **CHF 1,000,000** per incident
- **For drones 250g+:** Insurance is mandatory (CHF 1M minimum), and proof is required during FOCA registration
- **Critical issue for tourists:** Swiss insurance companies generally do NOT offer drone insurance to foreign tourists — arrange coverage in your home country that extends to Switzerland
- During dLIS registration for sub-250g drones, enter "dronebelow250g" in insurance fields — no policy number needed

**Practical note on the DJI Mini 4 Pro weight:**
The Mini 4 Pro weighs 249g WITHOUT accessories. With certain battery configurations or accessories, it may exceed 250g. If it registers as 250g+, full insurance becomes mandatory. Check your exact takeoff weight carefully.

**If you got German drone insurance:** Your German EU drone liability policy should cover operations in Switzerland (both are EASA states). Verify with your insurer that Switzerland is explicitly covered.

The sub-250g exemption gives you more options in areas OUTSIDE the valley, not within it.

- The interactive FOCA map is the single most important tool — check it for every specific spot you plan to fly from
- **NOTAMs change daily** — a spot that was legal yesterday could be restricted today due to temporary military exercises, rescue operations, or events

### 3.9 DJI GEO Zones / Geofencing in Switzerland

DJI's built-in geofencing system may restrict or warn about flights in certain areas:
- The DJI Fly app shows geo-restricted zones on its map (red = prohibited, yellow/blue = unlock required)
- You may need to self-unlock certain zones through the DJI app — this requires an online request with your DJI account
- **DJI geofencing is a SEPARATE system from Swiss legal restrictions** — a zone being "unlocked" in DJI does NOT mean it is legally permitted, and a zone NOT blocked by DJI does NOT mean it is legal
- Always cross-reference DJI zones with the official FOCA map
- Check DJI's GEO zone map for Switzerland before your trip: https://www.dji.com/flysafe/geo-map

### 3.10 Three-Step Pre-Flight Verification (Mandatory per FOCA)

FOCA requires drone pilots to complete this verification before EVERY flight:

1. **Step 1:** Check the interactive drone map at **map.geo.admin.ch** — view national and cantonal restriction areas for your exact GPS coordinates. Click on zones to see what authority manages them and what restrictions apply.
2. **Step 2:** Check the **Daily Airspace Bulletin Switzerland (DABS)** — updated multiple times daily with temporary restrictions and NOTAMs.
3. **Step 3:** Check for **areas with heightened collision risk** — heliports, mountain landing sites, shooting ranges, paragliding/hang-gliding zones. These are marked on the map but require careful attention.

### Privacy Laws
Switzerland uses its own privacy law (DSG/FADP, not GDPR). Article 13 FADP specifically addresses aerial photography — consent required to record identifiable persons.

### Penalties
- Standard violations: CHF 150 - 20,000
- Aggravated cases: up to CHF 40,000

## 4. ITALY

### Can tourists fly drones?
Yes, but with the most expensive registration of all four countries, and heavy restrictions around major cities.

### Registration Requirements (Updated March 2026)

**D-Flight registration is required for the DJI Mini 4 Pro** because it has a camera, even though it is sub-250g. The exemption from registration only applies to sub-250g drones WITHOUT cameras/sensors.

**Cost breakdown:**
- **D-Flight Base Subscription:** EUR 7/year (includes 1 QR code at first activation)
- **Additional Base QR Code:** EUR 7 per drone (if needed beyond the first)
- **ENAC operator registration fee:** EUR 90 (one-time, per some sources — but this is disputed)

**Note on the EUR 90 fee:** Multiple sources cite EUR 90 as the ENAC operator registration cost. However, the official D-Flight tariff page only lists EUR 7/year base subscription + EUR 7 per QR code. The EUR 90 may refer to an older fee structure or a specific declaration type. The situation is confusing even for Italian residents. **Budget EUR 90 to be safe, but the actual cost may be as low as EUR 14 (7+7).**

**Registration process for non-EU tourists:**
1. Create an account on d-flight.it (available in English)
2. For the "PEC" (certified email) field: leave unchecked if you don't have one
3. For the "C.F./CIF/T.I.N." (Italian tax number) field: enter "N/A"
4. Pay the subscription fee
5. Receive your operator ID / QR code — affix to drone
6. If already registered in another EU country (e.g., Germany via DIPUL), your e-ID may transfer automatically

**A1/A3 online training:** Required if drone is 250g+. For sub-250g recreational use, no pilot competency certificate is needed.

### Insurance

**The situation for sub-250g recreational drones is ambiguous:**
- The official ENAC position and main regulatory sources state insurance is **mandatory for all drones** including sub-250g
- However, some sources (including experienced local pilots) argue insurance is **not compulsory for hobby use with sub-250g drones**
- German law (LuftVG) and EU regulation both require insurance regardless of weight — Italy's implementation may differ slightly
- **Recommendation:** Get insurance anyway. Minimum coverage 750,000 SDR (~EUR 920,000). If you already have EU drone insurance from Germany registration, verify it covers Italy as well (most EU policies do).

### Detailed: Venice Lagoon Analysis (Updated March 2026)

**Q: Is the entire Venice lagoon banned or just the historic center?**

The situation is a layered prohibition:

1. **Venice historic center:** Complete 0m altitude ban on D-Flight — no drones at any altitude
2. **Venice Marco Polo Airport (VCE):** The airport CTR (Control Zone) covers a large area of the lagoon, extending over Murano and much of the northern lagoon. All drone flights in the CTR require ATC permission.
3. **Lido airfield:** Adds another restriction layer over the southern lagoon
4. **Burano:** About 99% is an orange zone with 25m maximum height. This means technically a flight under 25m might be legal in some spots, but:
   - You would need to be registered on D-Flight
   - The 25m limit produces poor-quality footage (too low for scenic shots)
   - Local enforcement may not distinguish between "25m allowed" and "banned"
   - Not practically useful
5. **Mestre (mainland):** Parts of Mestre fall under the airport CTR. Some areas outside the CTR boundary may be flyable — check D-Flight's interactive map for green zones. However, Mestre is urban, so you'd still face restrictions on flying over residential areas.

**Verdict: The entire Venice lagoon area is effectively unflyable for a tourist.** The combination of the historic center ban, airport CTR, and Lido airfield leaves virtually no usable airspace over the lagoon or islands.

### Detailed: Rome P-243/P-244 Analysis (Updated March 2026)

**Q: What are the exact boundaries of Rome's no-fly zones?**

Rome has TWO permanent prohibited zones:
- **P-243:** Covers Vatican City and immediate surroundings
- **P-244:** Covers Rome's core historical and government center

Both zones extend from the surface to approximately 3,500 feet (~1,067m). The no-fly area covers most of the city within and just around the Aurelian Walls (the ancient walls encircling the historic center).

**Landmarks confirmed within the restricted zones:** Colosseum, Roman Forum, Pantheon, Trevi Fountain, St. Peter's Basilica, Spanish Steps, Piazza Navona.

**Sub-250g exception?** No. The article from ts2.tech states explicitly: "The only exception is if you have a very lightweight drone <250g in subcategory A1, which technically may fly over people, but even then Rome's local rules still prohibit flying in most of the city." **Local prohibited zones override the EU sub-250g allowances.**

**Q: Are there spots just outside the P-244 zone?**

The exact boundary coordinates are only available on the D-Flight interactive map (requires login). The restricted zone roughly corresponds to the area within the GRA (Grande Raccordo Anulare / ring motorway) for the strictest restrictions, with the P-244 core covering the area within/near the Aurelian Walls.

**Potentially flyable areas near Rome:**
- Some suburbs well outside the historic center and outside airport CTRs (Fiumicino and Ciampino airports have their own large restriction zones)
- Rural areas along the Via Appia Antica (ancient Appian Way) — parts may be outside the core prohibited zone, but this is a heritage site with its own protections
- The countryside south or east of Rome, well outside the GRA

**Practically:** For your itinerary (focused on Colosseum, Vatican, central Rome), there are no flyable spots near your destinations. You would need to travel well outside the city.

### Detailed: Rural Italy Between Venice and Rome (Updated March 2026)

**Q: Can you fly in rural Tuscany or countryside areas from the train route?**

**Yes, rural Italy is the most promising opportunity.** The Venice-to-Rome train route (via Bologna and Florence) passes through:
- **Emilia-Romagna plains:** Flat agricultural land, generally fewer restrictions
- **Tuscan countryside:** Rolling hills, vineyards, Val d'Orcia — beautiful drone footage potential
- **Umbria:** Rural valleys and farmland

**Rules for rural flying in Italy:**
- Maximum 120m altitude
- Maintain visual line of sight
- Stay at least 50m from people, 150m from congested urban areas
- No flights over crowds
- No night flying
- Check D-Flight map for each specific location — scattered restrictions exist even in rural areas (military zones, small airfields, nature reserves)
- D-Flight uses color coding: green = flyable, orange = limited altitude (25/45/60m), red = prohibited

**Practical challenge:** You are traveling by train, not car. To fly in the Tuscan countryside, you would need to:
1. Stop in a small town (e.g., Orvieto, Montepulciano, or a town along the Florence-Rome route)
2. Walk or taxi to an open area away from the town center
3. Check D-Flight for that exact location
4. Fly, then continue your journey

This is only practical if you deliberately plan a countryside stop. The train windows are not a launching platform.

### Penalties
- Flying in prohibited zones: **up to 2 years imprisonment + fines up to EUR 64,000**
- Criminal offenses under Italy's navigation code
- Venice and Rome authorities actively enforce drone bans

### Where You COULD Realistically Fly

- **Emilia-Romagna farmland** (plains between Bologna and Florence)
- **Rural Umbria** (between Orvieto and Rome)
- Dolomites (outside protected zones — not on your route)
- Coastal areas away from airports (not on your route)
- **For your itinerary:** Venice = banned, Rome = banned. Only possible if you plan a deliberate countryside stop between the two cities.
